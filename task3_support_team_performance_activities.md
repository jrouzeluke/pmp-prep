# ACTIVITY 5: FEEDBACK TYPE MATCHER
## 12 Situations â†’ Match to Approach

**Instructions:** For each situation, match it to the appropriate feedback PURPOSE, TIMING, and DELIVERY.

**Feedback Categories:**

| Dimension | Options |
|-----------|---------|
| **Purpose** | Positive (reinforce), Coaching (develop), Constructive (redirect), Corrective (formal) |
| **Timing** | Immediate, Within 24 hours, Scheduled 1-on-1, Formal meeting |
| **Delivery** | Public, Private, Written follow-up |

---

### Situation 1
A developer just presented a flawless demo to the client who was visibly impressed. The team meeting just ended.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive**
- Timing: **Immediate**
- Delivery: **Public** (in team channel or next standup), **then private** for more detail

---

### Situation 2
A team member's code caused a production outage. This is the third incident this quarter. Previous informal conversations haven't changed behavior.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Corrective**
- Timing: **Within 24 hours, scheduled meeting**
- Delivery: **Private** with **written follow-up** (documented improvement plan)

---

### Situation 3
A junior developer asks good questions but tends to go down rabbit holes, spending too much time on low-priority details.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Coaching**
- Timing: **Scheduled 1-on-1**
- Delivery: **Private**

---

### Situation 4
A team member interrupted a colleague during their presentation, twice.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Constructive**
- Timing: **Within 24 hours** (after the meeting, not during)
- Delivery: **Private**

---

### Situation 5
A developer stayed late three nights to fix a critical bug before release. No one else knows except you.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive**
- Timing: **Within 24 hours**
- Delivery: **Ask preference** â€” some want public recognition, others prefer private thanks

---

### Situation 6
A team member missed a deadline for the second time, but quality was excellent when delivered. You haven't discussed this pattern yet.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Coaching**
- Timing: **Scheduled 1-on-1**
- Delivery: **Private**

Note: This is coaching, not corrective, because you haven't addressed it before. Start with understanding, not formality.

---

### Situation 7
A senior developer mentored a junior teammate through a complex problem. You observed it in a pairing session.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive**
- Timing: **Immediate or within 24 hours**
- Delivery: **Public** (if they're comfortable) â€” reinforces desired behavior for the whole team

---

### Situation 8
A team member sent a frustrated email to a stakeholder that was borderline unprofessional. The stakeholder forwarded it to you.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Constructive**
- Timing: **Within 24 hours**
- Delivery: **Private** â€” understand context first before judging

---

### Situation 9
Performance has improved significantly since your last coaching conversation. The team member is now meeting expectations.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive**
- Timing: **Next 1-on-1**
- Delivery: **Private** (specifically acknowledge the improvement)

---

### Situation 10
A new team member is struggling with the codebase after 3 weeks, making basic mistakes that others don't make.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Coaching**
- Timing: **Scheduled 1-on-1**
- Delivery: **Private**

Note: Investigate first. Is it ability (training needed), opportunity (no good documentation), or something else?

---

### Situation 11
You overhear a team member complaining about another teammate's work to a third person.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Constructive**
- Timing: **Within 24 hours**
- Delivery: **Private**

Address the behavior (talking about peers behind their back) and redirect to direct conversation or raising concerns with you.

---

### Situation 12
A team member has been on a documented performance improvement plan for 4 weeks with no progress despite support.

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Corrective** (escalation)
- Timing: **Formal scheduled meeting**
- Delivery: **Private** with **written documentation**, likely involving functional manager

# ACTIVITY 6: TEAM MEMBER PERSPECTIVES
## 5 Scenarios from the Receiving End

**Instructions:** Experience the same feedback delivered two different ways. Reflect on how each feels and what it makes you want to do.

---

## Scenario 1: The Missed Deadline

**Context:** You're a developer. You missed a deadline by one day because you discovered a security vulnerability that needed fixing. You didn't escalate itâ€”just fixed it. Your PM asks to talk.

---

### Experience Version A

Your PM says:
> "You missed the deadline. I had to explain to the stakeholders why we were late. This makes the whole team look bad. I need you to hit your deadlinesâ€”no excuses. If there's a problem, you need to tell me immediately, not just decide on your own what to do."

**How does this feel?**
- Defensive? Frustrated? Unappreciated?

**What do you want to do next?**
- Explain yourself? Give up? Just say "okay" and disengage?

---

### Experience Version B

Your PM says:
> "I saw you delivered a day late, and I noticed you fixed a security issue that wasn't in scope. Can you walk me through what happened? I want to understand the situation."

*You explain the vulnerability.*

> "Okay, I appreciate you catching thatâ€”it could have been a bigger problem later. In the future, when you find something like this, can you flag it to me right away? That way we can decide together whether to absorb the delay or adjust scope. I'll handle the stakeholder communication. Thanks for the quality focus."

**How does this feel?**
- Heard? Respected? Motivated to communicate earlier next time?

**What do you want to do next?**
- Flag issues earlier? Trust your PM more? Stay engaged?

---

### Reflection
Version A assumes bad intent. Version B assumes good intent and seeks to understand. Both address the real issue (escalation), but B does it without damage.

---

## Scenario 2: The Public Praise

**Context:** You're an introverted developer. In front of the whole team at standup, your PM says:

---

### Experience Version A

> "Everyone, I want to give a huge shout-out to [Your Name]! They absolutely crushed it on the authentication module. Best work I've seen in months! Can we get a round of applause?"

*Everyone claps. You feel your face getting red.*

**How does this feel?**
- Uncomfortable? Exposed? Wishing you could disappear?

---

### Experience Version B

*PM sends you a private message after standup:*

> "Hey, I wanted you to know the authentication module was excellent work. The way you handled the edge cases saved us from three potential bugs. Really appreciate the attention to detail. Is there anything you'd like me to highlight to the broader team, or would you prefer to keep this between us?"

**How does this feel?**
- Seen? Valued? Respected for who you are?

---

### Reflection
Recognition that ignores preferences can feel like punishment. Version B recognizes AND respects how you prefer to receive it.

---

## Scenario 3: The Performance Gap

**Context:** You've been struggling with a new technology stack. You know your output has been slow. No one has said anything for 3 weeks.

---

### Experience Version A

*Your PM schedules a meeting called "Performance Discussion." In the meeting:*

> "I've been tracking your velocity, and you're significantly behind the team average. You've had 3 weeks to ramp up and it's not happening. I need to see immediate improvement or we'll need to have a more formal conversation. I'm documenting this meeting."

**How does this feel?**
- Ambushed? Set up to fail? Anxious?

---

### Experience Version B

*In your regular 1-on-1:*

> "I wanted to check in on how you're feeling with the new stack. I've noticed the work is taking longer than expectedâ€”how's the ramp-up going from your perspective?"

*You share that you've been struggling but didn't want to admit it.*

> "Thanks for being honest. That's really helpful. Let's figure out some supportâ€”would pairing with Marcus help? He's worked with this stack for a year. I can also find some focused documentation. What would make the biggest difference?"

**How does this feel?**
- Supported? Safe to be honest? Motivated to improve?

---

### Reflection
Three weeks of silence followed by formal warning = ambush. Regular check-ins with genuine support = development.

---

## Scenario 4: The Constructive Feedback

**Context:** You interrupted a colleague twice during their presentation. You didn't realize it. After the meeting, your PM approaches.

---

### Experience Version A

> "You interrupted Sarah twice during her presentation. That's really disrespectful. You need to be more aware of how you come across. People are noticing."

**How does this feel?**
- Attacked? Labeled? Like your character is being judged?

---

### Experience Version B

> "Hey, can I share something I observed? During Sarah's presentation, you jumped in with comments twice while she was mid-sentence. I don't think you realized it, but she lost her train of thought both times. I know you're engaged and have great ideasâ€”can you hold them until she's finished and then build on what she said?"

**How does this feel?**
- Informed? Given a path to do better? Respected?

---

### Reflection
"You're disrespectful" is a character judgment. "You interrupted twice, here's the impact" is behavioral and actionable.

## Scenario 5: The Silent Treatment

**Context:** You've been doing solid, consistent work for 6 months. You haven't received any feedbackâ€”positive or constructive. Radio silence.

---

### Experience Version A

*Six months pass. Nothing. Then your annual review:*

> "You meet expectations. Keep doing what you're doing."

**How does this feel?**
- Invisible? Wondering if anyone notices? Like it doesn't matter if you stay or go?

---

### Experience Version B

*Over six months, you receive:*
- A specific thank-you for catching a bug before release
- A 1-on-1 where your PM asks about your career goals
- An invitation to lead a small initiative
- Recognition in a team meeting for helping a junior developer
- A note when your suggestion improved the process

**How does this feel?**
- Seen? Valued? Like your contributions matter? Like you want to stay?

---

### Reflection
Solid performers are often neglected because they don't cause problems. But silence breeds disengagement. The small moments of recognition and development attention are what retain good people.

| What They Need | What Damages It |
