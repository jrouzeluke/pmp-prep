Deep Dive

Comprehensive Guide to Team Leadership in Project Management

Introduction

Leading a team effectively is one of the most critical competencies for project managers. This deep dive explores two foundational frameworks that guide effective team leadership: Tuckman's Stages of Team Development and Goleman's Leadership Styles. Understanding when and how to apply these frameworks enables project managers to maximize team performance, navigate challenges, and deliver successful outcomes.

---

## Part 1: Tuckman's Stages of Team Development

### Historical Context

Dr. Bruce Tuckman introduced this model in 1965 based on his research into group dynamics. The model originally had four stages (Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing) and was later expanded in 1977 to include a fifth stage (Adjourning). This model has become the most widely recognized framework for understanding team development in organizational settings.

### Why This Model Matters

Teams don't automatically perform at high levels. They evolve through predictable stages, each with distinct characteristics and challenges. Understanding these stages allows project managers to:
- **Anticipate challenges** before they become critical
- **Adapt leadership approach** to what the team needs at each stage
- **Accelerate development** by addressing stage-specific needs
- **Recognize regression** when team composition or context changes
- **Set realistic expectations** for team performance timelines

---

### Stage 1: Forming

#### Overview
Forming is the orientation stage where team members meet, understand the project, and begin establishing relationships. This stage is characterized by politeness, uncertainty, and high dependence on the project manager for direction.

#### Detailed Characteristics

**Team Member Behaviors:**
- Polite and tentative with each other
- Avoid conflict or controversial topics
- Seek approval from PM and each other
- Ask many questions about roles, processes, and expectations
- Test boundaries cautiously
- Focus on understanding the task
- Socially anxious about fitting in
- Reluctant to share opinions openly

**Team Dynamics:**
- Little agreement except on broad objectives provided by PM
- Individual roles unclear or uncertain
- Low productivity (focused on orientation, not output)
- High dependence on PM for structure and direction
- Limited team identity or cohesion
- Communication is guarded and formal
- No established processes or norms

**Common Concerns Team Members Have:**
- "Will I fit in with this group?"
- "What exactly is my role?"
- "Who has what authority?"
- "What are the standards and expectations?"
- "Can I trust these people?"
- "Will my contributions be valued?"

#### PM Leadership Actions

**Primary Leadership Style:** Authoritative/Commanding
- Provide clear structure and direction
- Don't expect self-organization yet
- Be visible and available
- Make decisions confidently

**Specific Actions to Take:**

1. **Establish Structure**
   - Conduct kickoff meeting with clear agenda
   - Create and communicate project charter
   - Define roles and responsibilities explicitly
   - Establish reporting relationships
   - Set up communication channels and protocols

2. **Facilitate Relationship Building**
   - Organize team-building activities
   - Create opportunities for informal interaction
   - Facilitate introductions (skills, backgrounds, strengths)
   - Share personal information about yourself appropriately
   - Encourage team members to share about themselves

3. **Set Clear Expectations**
   - Define project goals and success criteria
   - Establish ground rules collaboratively
   - Clarify decision-making processes
   - Define quality standards
   - Set behavioral expectations

4. **Build Initial Trust**
   - Be consistent and reliable
   - Follow through on commitments
   - Demonstrate competence and confidence
   - Show respect for all team members
   - Create psychological safety for questions

5. **Provide Direction**
   - Answer questions patiently and thoroughly
   - Make decisions without expecting team consensus
   - Provide templates, examples, and guidance
   - Don't delegate prematurely
   - Check understanding frequently

#### Duration and Success Indicators

**Typical Duration:** 1-4 weeks for new project teams

**Factors Affecting Duration:**
- Prior relationships among team members (shorter if known each other)
- Complexity of project (more complex = longer forming)
- PM's effectiveness in providing structure
- Organizational culture and norms
- Team size (larger teams take longer)
- Clarity of objectives and roles

**Signs Team is Ready to Move to Storming:**
- Team members start expressing opinions more freely
- Questions shift from "what" to "how" and "why"
- Members challenge suggestions or approaches
- Informal coalitions or subgroups begin forming
- Comfort level increases (less politeness, more authenticity)

#### Common Mistakes in Forming Stage

❌ **Rushing Through Forming**
- Mistake: Skipping relationship building to "get to work"
- Consequence: Trust deficits cause problems later
- Better Approach: Invest time upfront in team formation

❌ **Expecting Self-Organization Too Soon**
- Mistake: Delegating decisions team isn't ready to make
- Consequence: Confusion, anxiety, poor decisions
- Better Approach: Provide structure, gradually increase autonomy

❌ **Not Addressing Logistics**
- Mistake: Assuming everyone knows how things work
- Consequence: Wasted time, missed meetings, confusion
- Better Approach: Explicitly establish all operational details

❌ **Allowing Ambiguity in Roles**
- Mistake: Vague job descriptions or overlapping responsibilities
- Consequence: Gaps, duplicated work, territorial conflicts
- Better Approach: Crystal clear role definition with RACI or similar

### Stage 2: Storming

#### Overview
Storming is the conflict stage where team members push against boundaries, compete for position, and challenge the PM's authority and each other's ideas. This stage is often uncomfortable but necessary for team development.

#### Detailed Characteristics

**Team Member Behaviors:**
- Challenge PM's decisions and authority
- Compete for influence and position
- Form coalitions or cliques
- Express frustrations openly (sometimes aggressively)
- Resist group norms or processes
- Focus on differences rather than similarities
- Test boundaries of acceptable behavior
- May question project value or approach

**Team Dynamics:**
- Increased conflict over methods, priorities, and responsibilities
- Power struggles emerge
- Communication becomes more direct (sometimes too direct)
- Subgroups with competing agendas
- Emotional reactions to disagreements
- Productivity often drops as energy goes to conflict
- Team may question whether they can succeed together
- Trust is tested

**Why Storming Happens:**
- Reality sets in (initial politeness wears off)
- Work pressures create stress
- Different working styles clash
- Competition for limited resources or recognition
- Unclear authority boundaries
- Personality differences become apparent
- Different values or priorities emerge
- Natural resistance to structure and norms

#### PM Leadership Actions

**Primary Leadership Style:** Coaching (with some Affiliative)
- Facilitate conflict resolution
- Don't force premature consensus
- Allow healthy debate
- Focus on team development, not just tasks

**Specific Actions to Take:**

1. **Acknowledge and Normalize Conflict**
   - Recognize Storming as healthy and necessary
   - Don't overreact to disagreements
   - Frame conflict as "working through how we'll succeed together"
   - Maintain calm demeanor (don't get defensive)
   - Communicate that this stage is temporary

2. **Facilitate Constructive Conflict**
   - Create safe forums for disagreement
   - Establish ground rules for respectful debate
   - Focus discussions on interests, not positions
   - Use structured problem-solving techniques
   - Ensure all voices are heard
   - Don't let conflicts become personal attacks

3. **Reinforce Vision and Goals**
   - Regularly reconnect team to shared objectives
   - Highlight common ground
   - Celebrate small wins together
   - Remind team why project matters
   - Keep focus on external goals, not internal dynamics

4. **Provide Coaching**
   - Individual conversations with struggling team members
   - Help members understand others' perspectives
   - Teach conflict resolution skills
   - Model desired behaviors
   - Give feedback on destructive behaviors

5. **Maintain Structure While Being Flexible**
   - Keep essential processes in place
   - Be open to improving approaches
   - Don't be rigid about "my way or the highway"
   - Involve team in process refinement
   - Balance stability with adaptability

6. **Build Relationships**
   - Increase team-building activities
   - Create informal interaction opportunities
   - Facilitate one-on-one connections
   - Share meals or social time if possible
   - Help team members see each other as humans

#### Duration and Success Indicators

**Typical Duration:** 3-8 weeks (can be longer for complex projects or challenging team dynamics)

**Factors Affecting Duration:**
- PM's skill in facilitating conflict
- Severity of personality or style differences
- Clarity of project goals and roles
- External pressures and deadlines
- Leadership support from sponsor
- Prior team experiences with conflict

**Teams May Cycle Back to Storming When:**
- New members join
- Significant project changes occur
- External pressures intensify
- Leadership changes
- Major setbacks or failures

**Signs Team is Ready to Move to Norming:**
- Conflicts become less frequent and less intense
- Team can resolve disagreements without PM intervention
- Focus shifts from "who's right" to "what's best for project"
- Humor and bonding emerge
- Members start supporting each other
- "We" language appears more frequently

#### Common Mistakes in Storming Stage

❌ **Avoiding or Suppressing Conflict**
- Mistake: Trying to smooth over disagreements or force harmony
- Consequence: Conflict goes underground, lengthens Storming stage
- Better Approach: Facilitate healthy conflict, address issues directly

❌ **Taking Sides in Conflicts**
- Mistake: Favoring one party over another
- Consequence: Lost credibility, increased division
- Better Approach: Stay neutral, focus on interests and solutions

❌ **Using Commanding Style Excessively**
- Mistake: Forcing decisions to end debates quickly
- Consequence: Resentment, lack of buy-in, suppressed conflict
- Better Approach: Facilitate discussion, build consensus when possible

❌ **Becoming Defensive**
- Mistake: Taking challenges to authority personally
- Consequence: Power struggles with PM, damaged relationships
- Better Approach: Welcome feedback, stay composed, focus on goals

❌ **Giving Up on the Team**
- Mistake: Concluding team can't work together, requesting replacements
- Consequence: Lost opportunity for high performance, reputation damage
- Better Approach: Persist through Storming with appropriate support

---

### Stage 3: Norming

#### Overview
Norming is the cohesion stage where team members establish working agreements, develop mutual respect, and begin functioning as a unit. The focus shifts from internal dynamics to external goals.

#### Detailed Characteristics

**Team Member Behaviors:**
- Actively cooperate with each other
- Give and receive constructive feedback
- Support each other's efforts
- Share responsibility for team success
- Flexible and open to others' perspectives
- Socialize and enjoy each other's company
- Take pride in being part of the team

**Team Dynamics:**
- Agreement on shared processes and norms
- Clear and accepted roles
- "We" language dominant ("our approach," "we decided")
- Mutual respect and trust developing
- Constructive disagreements (resolved collaboratively)
- Team identity emerges
- Productivity increases
- Information flows freely

**What's Different from Storming:**
- Conflicts are about work, not personal
- Disagreements resolved constructively
- Team self-corrects when someone violates norms
- Focus is external (project goals) not internal (team dynamics)
- Energy goes to output, not conflict management

#### PM Leadership Actions

**Primary Leadership Style:** Democratic/Participative
- Involve team in decisions
- Step back strategically
- Facilitate rather than direct
- Build on team's growing capability

**Specific Actions to Take:**

1. **Reduce Directive Leadership**
   - Transition from telling to asking
   - Seek team input before deciding
   - Let team drive discussions and decisions
   - Intervene less frequently
   - Trust team's judgment more

2. **Facilitate Collaboration**
   - Create opportunities for team problem-solving
   - Encourage peer-to-peer communication
   - Support collaborative work arrangements
   - Remove obstacles to teamwork
   - Celebrate collaborative successes

3. **Reinforce Positive Behaviors**
   - Recognize collaboration publicly
   - Praise team cohesion and mutual support
   - Highlight effective team processes
   - Share positive feedback from stakeholders
   - Document what's working well

4. **Maintain Momentum**
   - Keep team focused on goals
   - Ensure workload is balanced
   - Monitor for signs of regression
   - Address small issues before they grow
   - Continue regular team touchpoints

5. **Continue Development**
   - Provide coaching on skills
   - Support learning and growth
   - Cross-train team members
   - Prepare team for Performing stage
   - Increase complexity of delegated work

6. **Avoid Disrupting Working Norms**
   - Think carefully before changing processes
   - Involve team in any changes
   - Don't add unnecessary complexity
   - Respect team's established rhythms
   - Protect team from organizational turbulence

#### Duration and Success Indicators

**Typical Duration:** Can be sustained for months with proper maintenance

**Signs of Healthy Norming:**
- Team resolves conflicts without PM involvement
- Processes run smoothly
- Information sharing is automatic
- Team helps each other without being asked
- Quality of work is consistently good
- Team makes good decisions together

**Risks in Norming Stage:**
- **Complacency:** Team becomes comfortable, stops improving
- **Groupthink:** Desire for harmony suppresses good ideas
- **Insularity:** Team becomes closed to outside input
- **Conflict Avoidance:** Fear of returning to Storming prevents healthy debate

**Signs Team is Ready for Performing:**
- Sustained high performance over time
- Team anticipates and prevents problems
- Self-correction without PM involvement
- Innovation and continuous improvement
- High trust and psychological safety

#### Common Mistakes in Norming Stage

❌ **Returning to Directive Leadership**
- Mistake: Reverting to Forming/Storming style behaviors
- Consequence: Undermines team development, damages trust
- Better Approach: Continue stepping back, delegate more

❌ **Disrupting Working Norms Unnecessarily**
- Mistake: Changing processes that are working
- Consequence: Confusion, frustration, possible regression
- Better Approach: Involve team in any changes, change only when needed

❌ **Taking Credit for Team Success**
- Mistake: Positioning yourself as the reason for success
- Consequence: Resentment, reduced motivation
- Better Approach: Give credit to team, celebrate their capability

❌ **Assuming Team Has "Arrived"**
- Mistake: Neglecting team maintenance and development
- Consequence: Regression or stagnation
- Better Approach: Continue investing in team relationships and growth

### Stage 4: Performing

#### Overview
Performing is the peak performance stage where teams operate with high autonomy, deliver exceptional results, and function as a cohesive, self-managing unit. This is the goal state that effective leadership enables.

#### Detailed Characteristics

**Team Member Behaviors:**
- Take initiative without prompting
- Solve problems independently
- Help each other proactively
- Hold each other accountable
- Make good decisions autonomously
- Focus on continuous improvement
- Share leadership naturally
- Demonstrate high commitment

**Team Dynamics:**
- High trust and psychological safety
- Efficient, effective processes
- Shared leadership emerges
- Disagreements resolved quickly and constructively
- High productivity and quality
- Innovation and creativity flourish
- Team makes most decisions without PM
- Clear sense of purpose and identity
- Pride in team accomplishments

**What Makes Performing Different:**
- **Autonomy:** Team doesn't need PM for day-to-day decisions
- **Proactivity:** Team anticipates and prevents problems
- **Resilience:** Team handles setbacks without falling apart
- **Excellence:** Consistently high-quality outputs
- **Growth:** Continuous improvement mindset
- **Synergy:** Whole is greater than sum of parts

#### PM Leadership Actions

**Primary Leadership Style:** Delegating
- Minimal intervention in day-to-day work
- Focus on strategic issues
- Remove obstacles
- Trust team's capability

**Specific Actions to Take:**

1. **Empower and Trust**
   - Delegate meaningful authority
   - Allow team to self-organize
   - Step in only when asked or truly necessary
   - Support team's decisions even when you'd decide differently
   - Give team credit and visibility

2. **Remove Strategic Obstacles**
   - Focus on organizational barriers
   - Secure resources and support
   - Manage stakeholder expectations
   - Shield team from unnecessary distractions
   - Escalate issues beyond team's control

3. **Challenge and Stretch**
   - Set ambitious goals
   - Encourage innovation
   - Support calculated risk-taking
   - Provide growth opportunities
   - Increase complexity of delegated work

4. **Maintain Alignment**
   - Ensure team stays connected to organizational strategy
   - Communicate changes in direction or priorities
   - Connect team to broader organizational goals
   - Share relevant external information
   - Keep team informed of their impact

5. **Celebrate and Recognize**
   - Publicly acknowledge team excellence
   - Ensure team gets organizational recognition
   - Celebrate milestones and achievements
   - Share positive feedback from stakeholders
   - Reward team appropriately

6. **Prepare for Transition**
   - Document lessons learned continuously
   - Build organizational capabilities
   - Plan for knowledge transfer
   - Develop succession for key roles
   - Think about project closure or transition

#### Duration and Success Indicators

**Duration:** Can be sustained indefinitely with stable team and proper maintenance

**Characteristics of High-Performing Teams:**
- Deliver exceptional results consistently
- Innovate and improve continuously
- Handle crises and setbacks resiliently
- Self-correct without external intervention
- Maintain high morale and engagement
- Develop members' capabilities
- Achieve stretch goals

**Threats to Performing Stage:**
- Major team composition changes
- Significant organizational changes
- Loss of key members
- Extended periods of crisis or pressure
- Lack of recognition or support
- Changing project scope or goals dramatically

#### Common Mistakes in Performing Stage

❌ **Micromanaging**
- Mistake: Reverting to directive leadership with capable team
- Consequence: Demotivation, resentment, reduced performance
- Better Approach: Continue trusting and empowering

❌ **Taking Team for Granted**
- Mistake: Assuming performance will continue without support
- Consequence: Burnout, disengagement, departure of key members
- Better Approach: Continue recognition, development, and care

❌ **Introducing Unnecessary Change**
- Mistake: Fixing what isn't broken
- Consequence: Disruption, confusion, possible regression
- Better Approach: Change only when clearly beneficial, involve team

❌ **Not Preparing for Closure**
- Mistake: No planning for project end or transition
- Consequence: Chaotic closure, knowledge loss, team stress
- Better Approach: Begin closure planning well in advance

### Stage 5: Adjourning (or Mourning)

#### Overview
Adjourning is the dissolution stage when the project ends and the team disbands. This stage involves closure activities, emotional processing, and transition planning.

#### Detailed Characteristics

**Team Member Behaviors:**
- Reflect on accomplishments and experiences
- Express gratitude to each other
- Feel sadness or loss about ending
- Anxiety about future assignments
- Nostalgia about team experiences
- Desire to stay connected
- Pride in what was accomplished
- Variable engagement (some check out early)

**Team Dynamics:**
- Focus shifts from project work to closure
- Productivity may decline slightly
- Emotional processing becomes important
- Relationships and connections emphasized
- Celebration and recognition
- Knowledge transfer and documentation
- Reduced urgency about ongoing work

**Emotional Reactions Vary:**
- **Relief:** For difficult or stressful projects
- **Sadness:** For strong, positive team experiences
- **Anxiety:** About what's next professionally
- **Pride:** In accomplishments achieved together
- **Loss:** Of daily connection and relationships
- **Excitement:** About new opportunities

#### PM Leadership Actions

**Primary Leadership Style:** Coaching/Affiliative (Supporting)
- Facilitate proper closure
- Support emotional needs
- Ensure knowledge transfer
- Celebrate accomplishments

**Specific Actions to Take:**

1. **Facilitate Proper Closure**
   - Conduct comprehensive project retrospective
   - Capture lessons learned thoroughly
   - Complete all deliverables and documentation
   - Archive project materials appropriately
   - Close contracts and financial accounts
   - Formal acceptance and sign-off

2. **Celebrate Achievements**
   - Host closing celebration or ceremony
   - Recognize individual contributions publicly
   - Thank team members specifically and personally
   - Share success stories with organization
   - Provide performance recognition
   - Create team memory artifacts (photos, stories)

3. **Support Transition**
   - Assist team members with next assignments
   - Provide references or recommendations
   - Share information about future opportunities
   - Help process emotions about ending
   - Maintain communication during transition
   - Check in on team members after project ends

4. **Ensure Knowledge Transfer**
   - Document processes, decisions, and lessons
   - Train operational or maintenance teams
   - Create comprehensive handoff materials
   - Record tacit knowledge and expertise
   - Complete lessons learned repository
   - Share best practices with organization

5. **Maintain Relationships**
   - Exchange contact information
   - Plan reunion or follow-up
   - Connect via professional networks
   - Stay available for questions
   - Build lasting professional relationships
   - Reflect on growth and development

#### Duration and Success Indicators

**Typical Duration:** 2-4 weeks depending on project length and team bonding

**Signs of Healthy Adjourning:**
- All deliverables completed and accepted
- Lessons learned captured comprehensively
- Team members have clear next steps
- Relationships preserved positively
- Knowledge successfully transferred
- Appropriate closure ceremony held
- Emotional needs addressed

#### Common Mistakes in Adjourning Stage

❌ **Rushing Through Closure**
- Mistake: Disbanding team immediately after last deliverable
- Consequence: Knowledge loss, unresolved emotions, incomplete closure
- Better Approach: Plan adequate time for proper closure activities

❌ **Failing to Acknowledge Contributions**
- Mistake: No recognition or celebration of team
- Consequence: Resentment, reduced future commitment, missed morale opportunity
- Better Approach: Recognize individuals and team meaningfully

❌ **Not Capturing Lessons Learned**
- Mistake: Skipping retrospective or documentation
- Consequence: Organizational learning lost, same mistakes repeated
- Better Approach: Thorough lessons learned process with action items

❌ **Ignoring Emotional Aspects**
- Mistake: Treating ending as purely transactional
- Consequence: Team members feel undervalued, relationships damaged
- Better Approach: Acknowledge emotions, provide space for processing

## Part 2: Goleman's Six Leadership Styles

### Background and Research

Daniel Goleman, psychologist and author, identified six leadership styles based on research into emotional intelligence and organizational leadership. His research, published in Harvard Business Review (2000), demonstrated that leaders who master multiple styles and can flex between them achieve better results than those who rely on one approach.

### Why Leadership Style Matters

Research shows that leadership style has **direct impact on organizational climate** (working environment, morale, commitment), which in turn affects **performance outcomes** (productivity, quality, retention).

**Key Research Findings:**
- Leaders who use 4+ styles have **best organizational climate**
- Most effective leaders **flex between styles** based on situation
- Climate accounts for **~30% of business performance**
- Authoritative style has **most positive overall impact**
- Commanding style has **most negative overall impact**
- All styles have appropriate use cases

---

### Style 1: Commanding (Coercive, Directive)

#### Overview
"Do what I tell you" - The leader makes decisions unilaterally, gives orders, and expects immediate compliance.

#### When to Use

✓ **Crisis Situations**
- Emergency requiring immediate action
- Safety issues
- Security breaches
- Production failures affecting customers
- Regulatory violations

✓ **Dealing with Problem Employees**
- After coaching has failed
- When termination is imminent
- Serious policy violations
- Willful non-compliance

✓ **Turnaround Situations**
- Failing project needs quick correction
- Team has lost direction completely
- Previous approaches have failed
- Urgent restructuring needed

✓ **Military/Emergency Services Context**
- Situations where hesitation risks lives
- Time for discussion does not exist
- Chain of command is essential

#### Characteristics

**How It Looks:**
- Clear, direct orders
- No explanation of rationale
- Minimal input from others
- Focus on immediate compliance
- Tight control and oversight
- Consequences for non-compliance
- Fast decision-making

**Impact on Team Climate:**
- **Most negative long-term impact** of all styles
- Reduces flexibility (team can't adapt)
- Damages responsibility (team becomes dependent)
- Lowers commitment (people don't own work)
- Stifles innovation and creativity
- Can increase fear and anxiety

**When It Works Well:**
- Team understands urgency
- Crisis is obvious to all
- Used temporarily
- Followed by other styles
- Team knows it's exceptional

#### Common Mistakes

❌ **Using as Default Style**
- Creates dependent, unmotivated team
- Reduces initiative and creativity
- Damages long-term relationships
- Limits team development

❌ **Over-Explaining Why**
- In true crisis, explanation wastes time
- Can appear indecisive
- Mixed message (commanding or not?)

❌ **Continuing After Crisis Passes**
- Damages relationships unnecessarily
- Limits team capability development
- Reduces future performance

#### Best Practices

✓ Use sparingly and temporarily
✓ Explain the "why" after crisis passes
✓ Transition to collaborative styles ASAP
✓ Apologize if style was unnecessarily harsh
✓ Rebuild relationships after using
✓ Reserve for true emergencies only

---

### Style 2: Authoritative (Visionary)

#### Overview
"Come with me" - The leader provides clear vision and direction while allowing team autonomy in how they achieve goals.

#### When to Use

✓ **New Direction Needed**
- Project starting
- Strategy shift
- Market changes
- New opportunities emerging

✓ **Team Lacks Clarity**
- Confusion about goals
- Lack of alignment
- Competing priorities
- Mission drift

✓ **Change Management**
- Organizational restructuring
- New methodology adoption
- Cultural transformation
- Major process changes

✓ **Building Consensus**
- Diverse team needs alignment
- Stakeholder confusion
- Multiple competing perspectives

#### Characteristics

**How It Looks:**
- Articulate compelling vision
- Explain the "why" clearly
- Set direction, not methods
- Inspire and motivate
- Build confidence
- Connect daily work to bigger purpose
- Allow flexibility in execution

**Impact on Team Climate:**
- **Most positive overall impact**
- Maximizes commitment
- Provides clarity
- Builds confidence
- Encourages innovation within boundaries
- Creates sense of purpose

**When It Works Well:**
- Leader has credibility
- Vision is compelling
- Team is competent enough to execute
- Direction is genuinely needed
- Leader can articulate vision well

#### Common Mistakes

❌ **Using with Highly Expert Teams**
- Experts don't need vision from someone less expert
- Can seem condescending
- May be rejected if vision seems uninformed

❌ **Vision is Vague or Unconvincing**
- Generic statements don't inspire
- Lack of specificity causes confusion
- Vision disconnected from reality

❌ **Not Allowing Autonomy**
- Providing vision then micromanaging "how"
- Contradicts the style's premise
- Reduces motivation

#### Best Practices

✓ Develop clear, compelling vision first
✓ Communicate "why" before "what"
✓ Connect individual roles to vision
✓ Allow team to determine "how"
✓ Share your passion and enthusiasm
✓ Check that vision resonates
✓ Adjust based on feedback

---

### Style 3: Affiliative (Relationship-Oriented)

#### Overview
"People come first" - The leader prioritizes harmony, relationships, and emotional well-being over tasks.

#### When to Use

✓ **Team is Under Stress**
- High-pressure periods
- Difficult work conditions
- External challenges
- Personal struggles affecting work

✓ **Trust Has Been Broken**
- After conflict
- Following layoffs or reorganization
- New leader joining
- Major disappointments

✓ **Building Team Cohesion**
- New team forming
- After Storming stage
- Distributed team needing connection
- Low morale

✓ **After Traumatic Events**
- Project failure
- Team member departure
- Organizational crisis
- External tragedies

#### Characteristics

**How It Looks:**
- Emphasis on emotional well-being
- Flexible on deadlines if people need it
- Frequent praise and encouragement
- Active listening to personal concerns
- Team-building activities
- Celebration of relationships
- Harmony prioritized over confrontation

**Impact on Team Climate:**
- Builds strong bonds
- Increases loyalty
- Creates psychological safety
- Improves morale significantly
- Enhances trust

**When It Works Well:**
- Used in combination with other styles
- Team needs emotional support
- Relationships actually are priority
- Balanced with accountability
- Temporary focus on people

#### Common Mistakes

❌ **Using Exclusively**
- Performance may suffer
- Accountability decreases
- Standards may slip
- Difficult conversations avoided

❌ **Avoiding All Conflict**
- Issues fester
- Problems don't get solved
- Can enable poor performance

❌ **Over-Praising**
- Recognition loses meaning
- Can seem insincere
- Grade inflation

❌ **Ignoring Performance Issues**
- Problem employees protected
- Team resentment builds
- Results suffer

#### Best Practices

✓ Combine with Authoritative or Democratic
✓ Don't avoid necessary difficult conversations
✓ Balance relationship focus with results
✓ Use strategically when relationships need repair
✓ Show genuine care and concern
✓ Follow up on personal situations

---

### Style 4: Democratic (Participative)

#### Overview
"What do you think?" - The leader actively seeks input, builds consensus, and makes decisions collaboratively.

#### When to Use

✓ **Need Team Buy-In**
- Significant changes
- New processes
- Strategy decisions
- Resource allocation

✓ **Team Has Expertise**
- Technical decisions
- Process design
- Quality standards
- Tool selection

✓ **Complex Problems**
- Multiple perspectives needed
- No obvious solution
- High uncertainty
- Innovative thinking required

✓ **Building Capability**
- Developing team's decision-making
- Increasing engagement
- Building ownership

#### Characteristics

**How It Looks:**
- Frequent team meetings
- Soliciting opinions actively
- Voting or consensus-building
- Explaining how input influenced decision
- Shared decision-making
- Lengthy discussions
- Team feels heard and valued

**Impact on Team Climate:**
- Increases flexibility
- Builds responsibility
- Enhances commitment
- Improves morale
- Develops capability

**When It Works Well:**
- Team is competent and informed
- Time available for discussion
- Team's input will genuinely influence decision
- Multiple valid perspectives exist
- Implementation requires team commitment

#### Common Mistakes

❌ **Using in Emergencies**
- Wastes precious time
- Appears indecisive
- Creates confusion

❌ **When Team Lacks Expertise**
- Uninformed opinions don't help
- Can lead to poor decisions
- Team recognizes futility

❌ **Consensus as Excuse for Indecision**
- Never making final decision
- Endless debate
- Analysis paralysis

❌ **Not Deciding After Getting Input**
- Team frustration
- Wasted time and effort
- Reduces future participation

#### Best Practices

✓ Be clear about decision-making process
✓ Set time limits for discussion
✓ Explain how input will be used
✓ Make decision after gathering input
✓ Thank people for their input
✓ Communicate the decision and rationale

---

### Style 5: Pacesetting

#### Overview
"Do as I do, now" - The leader sets extremely high standards, models them personally, and expects team to follow the example.

#### When to Use

✓ **Short-Term Push**
- Critical deadline approaching
- Demo or presentation imminent
- Emergency response after crisis
- Sprint to finish line

✓ **Highly Competent Team**
- Team can meet high standards
- Members are self-motivated
- Technical experts who thrive on challenge
- Results-oriented culture

✓ **Leading by Example**
- New process being introduced
- Demonstrating commitment
- Technical skill transfer

#### Characteristics

**How It Looks:**
- Leader works intensely
- High standards, often implicit
- Little patience for poor performance
- Leader often does work themselves
- Fast pace
- Achievement-focused
- Impatience with those who can't keep up

**Impact on Team Climate:**
- **Can damage climate if overused**
- May overwhelm team members
- Reduces flexibility (one way to do it)
- Can decrease morale
- May increase turnover
- Short-term performance boost

**When It Works Well:**
- Team has capabilities to meet standards
- Used temporarily
- Team is motivated by challenge
- Leader's example is inspiring not intimidating
- Clear endpoint exists

#### Common Mistakes

❌ **Using as Default Style**
- Burns out team
- Creates unsustainable pace
- Reduces morale over time
- Limits development of others

❌ **With Mixed-Ability Team**
- Some can't keep up
- Creates class system
- Demoralizes lower performers
- May lose good people

❌ **Not Providing Coaching**
- Just setting high bar without support
- Team doesn't develop
- Only leader can do it right

❌ **Continuing Too Long**
- Burnout inevitable
- Diminishing returns
- Health and relationship impacts

#### Best Practices

✓ Use for short bursts only
✓ Ensure team has capability
✓ Combine with Coaching
✓ Monitor for burnout signs
✓ Celebrate achievements
✓ Transition to sustainable pace quickly

---

### Style 6: Coaching (Developmental)

#### Overview
"Try this" - The leader focuses on developing team members for the future, providing feedback and growth opportunities.

#### When to Use

✓ **Developing Talent**
- Building long-term capabilities
- Career development
- Skill gaps identified
- Succession planning

✓ **Team Member Motivated to Learn**
- Requests for growth opportunities
- Career discussions
- New challenges needed
- Aspirational goals expressed

✓ **Time Available**
- Not under crisis pressure
- Can invest in development
- Learning is priority
- Can accept learning mistakes

✓ **Building Organizational Capability**
- Knowledge transfer needed
- Institutional knowledge at risk
- Capability gaps identified

#### Characteristics

**How It Looks:**
- Regular development conversations
- Delegating stretch assignments
- Providing detailed feedback
- Creating learning opportunities
- Tolerating mistakes as learning
- Future-focused discussions
- Investment in growth

**Impact on Team Climate:**
- Highly positive long-term
- Builds capability
- Increases engagement
- Improves retention
- Creates learning culture

**When It Works Well:**
- Team member is receptive
- Time available for development
- Leader has coaching skills
- Development opportunities exist
- Performance is adequate

#### Common Mistakes

❌ **Using During Crisis**
- Immediate results needed
- No time for development
- Learning mistakes unacceptable

❌ **With Unmotivated Individuals**
- Coaching requires willingness
- Can't force development
- Wastes effort

❌ **Coaching Without Follow-Up**
- Development discussions but no action
- No accountability for growth
- Seen as empty promises

❌ **Only Teaching, Not Learning**
- Not adapting to individual needs
- One approach for everyone
- Not learning from mentee

#### Best Practices

✓ Ensure individual wants development
✓ Set clear development goals
✓ Provide regular feedback
✓ Create learning opportunities
✓ Celebrate progress
✓ Balance development with delivery
✓ Follow up consistently

## Part 3: Situational Leadership - Matching Style to Situation

### Integration Framework

Effective leadership requires combining Tuckman's stages with appropriate leadership styles. Here's how they integrate:

**Forming Stage:**
- **Primary:** Authoritative (provide vision)
- **Secondary:** Commanding (provide structure)
- **Why:** Team needs clear direction and can't self-organize yet

**Storming Stage:**
- **Primary:** Coaching (facilitate through conflict)
- **Secondary:** Affiliative (repair relationships)
- **Why:** Team needs help working through disagreements constructively

**Norming Stage:**
- **Primary:** Democratic (involve in decisions)
- **Secondary:** Coaching (continue development)
- **Why:** Team ready to participate, needs continued growth

**Performing Stage:**
- **Primary:** Delegating (trust and empower)
- **Secondary:** Authoritative (strategic direction only)
- **Why:** Team capable of self-management, needs autonomy

**Adjourning Stage:**
- **Primary:** Affiliative (support emotions)
- **Secondary:** Coaching (reflect and learn)
- **Why:** Team needs closure support and knowledge capture

### Additional Situational Factors

Beyond team stage, consider:

**1. Urgency Level**
- **High urgency:** Commanding → Authoritative → Pacesetting
- **Medium urgency:** Authoritative → Democratic
- **Low urgency:** Democratic → Coaching

**2. Task Complexity**
- **High complexity:** Democratic (need multiple perspectives) + Coaching
- **Medium complexity:** Authoritative + Coaching
- **Low complexity:** Delegating or Pacesetting

**3. Team Member Competence (Individual)**
- **Low competence, low confidence:** Commanding + Coaching
- **Some competence, low confidence:** Coaching + Affiliative
- **High competence, low confidence:** Democratic + Affiliative
- **High competence, high confidence:** Delegating

**4. Organizational Culture**
- **Hierarchical:** Commanding and Authoritative more accepted
- **Collaborative:** Democratic and Coaching expected
- **Results-driven:** Pacesetting and Authoritative effective
- **Relationship-focused:** Affiliative and Democratic valued

**5. Stakeholder Expectations**
- **Command-and-control sponsors:** May expect Commanding
- **Collaborative sponsors:** Expect Democratic approach
- **Hands-off sponsors:** Delegating works well

### Style Transitions

**How to Shift Between Styles:**

1. **Explain the Change**
   - "Given the emergency, I need to be more directive right now"
   - "Now that crisis passed, let's return to collaborative approach"

2. **Be Intentional**
   - Consciously choose style
   - Don't default unconsciously
   - Match to situation

3. **Get Feedback**
   - "How is my leadership working for you?"
   - "What do you need more/less of from me?"
   - Adjust based on input

4. **Develop Range**
   - Practice unfamiliar styles
   - Observe others using different styles
   - Get coaching on weak areas
   - Expand comfort zone

## Summary and Key Takeaways

### Tuckman's Stages - Key Points

1. **Teams evolve through predictable stages**
   - Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning
   - Each stage has distinct characteristics
   - Regression possible with changes

2. **Each stage requires different leadership**
   - Forming: Structure and direction
   - Storming: Facilitation and coaching
   - Norming: Participation and empowerment
   - Performing: Delegation and support
   - Adjourning: Recognition and closure

3. **Not all teams reach Performing**
   - Requires effective leadership through each stage
   - Can get stuck in Storming without intervention
   - Maintaining Performing requires ongoing effort

### Leadership Styles - Key Points

1. **Six styles, all have appropriate uses**
   - Commanding: Emergencies only
   - Authoritative: Most positive overall
   - Affiliative: Relationship repair
   - Democratic: Complex decisions
   - Pacesetting: Short bursts with capable team
   - Coaching: Long-term development

2. **Flexibility is essential**
   - Match style to situation
   - Use multiple styles
   - Transition as needed
   - Develop full range

3. **Some styles overused, others underused**
   - Commanding overused (default under stress)
   - Coaching underused (takes time)
   - Affiliative underused (task focus dominates)

### Integration - Key Points

1. **Combine frameworks for best results**
   - Team stage + situation + individual needs
   - No single formula
   - Requires judgment

2. **Self-awareness is foundation**
   - Know your default style
   - Recognize your triggers
   - Understand your impact
   - Seek feedback

3. **Practice and reflection**
   - Try unfamiliar styles
   - Reflect on results
   - Adjust and improve
   - Continuous learning
