## Part 1: The Performance Support Framework

### Understanding Your Role

Project managers typically don't have formal authority over team members' performance reviews, compensation, or career progression. This creates a unique challenge: how do you improve performance without traditional management levers?

**The PM's Performance Toolkit:**
1. **Feedback** - Regular, specific, actionable input on performance

3. **Development** - Providing growth opportunities
4. **Coaching** - Helping team members improve
5. **Environment** - Creating conditions for success

### The Performance Equation

```
Performance = Ability √ó Motivation √ó Opportunity
```

- **Ability**: Skills, knowledge, competencies
- **Motivation**: Willingness, engagement, drive
- **Opportunity**: Resources, environment, support

When performance declines, diagnose which factor is the cause before selecting an intervention:

| Factor | Symptoms | Intervention |
|--------|----------|--------------|
| Ability Gap | Makes mistakes, asks many questions | Training, mentoring, pair work |
| Motivation Issue | Disengaged, minimal effort, late | Recognition, meaningful work, address concerns |
| Opportunity Blocker | Complains about obstacles, waits often | Remove impediments, provide resources |

## Part 2: The SBI Feedback Model

### What is SBI?

SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) is the gold standard for delivering feedback. It keeps feedback objective, specific, and actionable.

**S - Situation**
- When and where did the behavior occur?
- Be specific about time and place
- Creates shared context

**B - Behavior**
- What specific, observable behavior occurred?
- Focus on actions, not personality
- Avoid interpretations or judgments

**I - Impact**
- What was the effect of the behavior?
- On the project, team, stakeholders, customer
- Connect behavior to outcomes

### SBI Examples

**Constructive Feedback Example:**

‚ùå **Bad**: "You're always unprepared for meetings."

‚úÖ **Good SBI**: 
- **S**: "In yesterday's client demo..."
- **B**: "...you presented the feature without testing it first, and it crashed twice..."
- **I**: "...which made the client question our quality practices and delayed their sign-off decision by a week."

**Positive Feedback Example:**

‚ùå **Bad**: "Great job on the project."

‚úÖ **Good SBI**:
- **S**: "During last week's production incident..."
- **B**: "...you immediately took ownership, coordinated the response team, and communicated updates every 30 minutes to stakeholders..."
- **I**: "...which kept everyone calm, reduced resolution time by 2 hours, and the client specifically praised our incident response."

### SBI Delivery Tips

1. **Prepare**: Write out your SBI before the conversation
2. **Private Setting**: Constructive feedback always in private
3. **Timely**: Deliver as close to the event as possible
4. **Two-Way**: After delivering SBI, ask for their perspective
5. **Action-Oriented**: End with "What can we do differently next time?"

## Part 3: Recognition Strategies

### Why Recognition Matters

Research consistently shows that recognition is one of the most powerful performance drivers:
- 69% of employees would work harder if better recognized
- Recognition is the #1 driver of great work
- Lack of recognition is a top reason people leave jobs

### Types of Recognition

**By Formality:**

| Type | Examples | Best For |
|------|----------|----------|
| Formal | Awards, bonuses, promotions, certificates | Major achievements, milestone completions |
| Informal | Verbal praise, thank-you notes, shout-outs | Daily contributions, effort, improvement |

**By Visibility:**

| Type | Examples | Best For |
|------|----------|----------|
| Public | Team meeting recognition, company announcements | People who prefer visibility, team wins |
| Private | 1-on-1 praise, personal notes | Introverts, personal achievements |

### The Recognition Framework

**T.A.P.S. Model:**

- **T - Timely**: Recognize as close to the event as possible
- **A - Authentic**: Be genuine; people detect fake praise
- **P - Personal**: Match recognition to individual preferences
- **S - Specific**: State exactly what they did and why it mattered

### Recognition Best Practices

**Ask About Preferences**
At project start or in 1-on-1s, ask:
- "How do you prefer to receive recognition?"
- "Are you comfortable with public recognition?"
- "What types of recognition are most meaningful to you?"

**Recognize Different Things**
- **Results**: Achieving goals, deliverables, outcomes
- **Effort**: Hard work, extra hours, persistence
- **Improvement**: Growth, learning, skill development
- **Behaviors**: Collaboration, helping others, initiative
- **Ideas**: Innovation, problem-solving, suggestions

**Avoid Recognition Mistakes**
- Generic praise ("Good job") without specifics
- Recognizing only top performers (demotivates others)
- Public recognition for those who prefer private
- Delayed recognition (loses impact)
- Recognizing only results, not effort

## Part 4: Addressing Underperformance

### The Underperformance Process

**Step 1: Identify the Issue**
- What specific behaviors or outcomes are below expectations?
- How does it compare to past performance or peers?
- Is it consistent or situational?

**Step 2: Investigate Root Causes**
Before assuming the worst, explore:
- Personal issues (health, family, stress)
- Work issues (unclear expectations, inadequate resources)
- Skill gaps (new technology, changed requirements)
- Motivation issues (disengagement, burnout, conflict)
- Systemic issues (process problems, dependencies)

**Step 3: Have the Conversation**

Structure:
1. **Open**: "I want to discuss some observations and hear your perspective."
2. **Share**: Use SBI to describe specific performance gaps
3. **Listen**: "What's been happening from your perspective?"
4. **Explore**: "What do you think is contributing to this?"
5. **Plan**: "What can we do to address this together?"
6. **Commit**: "Let's agree on specific actions and check in again on [date]."

**Step 4: Create Improvement Plan**
- Specific, measurable goals
- Timeline for improvement
- Resources and support provided
- Check-in frequency
- Consequences if no improvement (be honest)

**Step 5: Follow Up**
- Monitor progress against plan
- Provide feedback during improvement period
- Recognize improvements
- Document conversations

**Step 6: Escalate if Necessary**
If improvement doesn't occur despite support:
- Document all coaching attempts
- Involve functional manager
- Consider reassignment or removal from project

### Coaching vs. Corrective Action

**Coaching** (First Response)
- Collaborative approach
- Focus on development
- Future-oriented
- Trust-building

**Counseling** (Escalation)
- More directive
- Focus on specific issues
- Sets clear expectations
- Documents concerns

**Corrective Action** (Last Resort)
- Formal process
- Clear consequences
- Documentation required
- HR involvement

### The Underperformance Conversation

**What to Say:**

"Alex, I want to have a candid conversation with you. Over the past three sprints, I've noticed that the features you've delivered have required significant rework‚Äîaveraging about 40% revision after code review. This is causing delays for the team and extra work for reviewers.

I want to understand what's happening from your perspective. Is there something going on that I should know about? Are there obstacles or gaps I can help address?

What I'd like is for us to work together on a plan to get things back on track. I believe you can do great work‚ÄîI've seen it before‚Äîand I want to support you in getting there."

**What NOT to Say:**

"Your work has been terrible lately. You need to do better or there will be consequences. I don't know what's wrong with you."

## Part 5: Development and Growth

### The Development Mindset

Great project managers see performance issues as development opportunities, not character flaws. This mindset:
- Assumes people want to succeed
- Focuses on capability building
- Creates psychological safety
- Improves long-term performance

### Creating Development Plans

**Components:**
1. **Current State**: Where are they now?
2. **Desired State**: Where do they need to be?
3. **Gap Analysis**: What's missing?
4. **Development Activities**: How will they get there?
5. **Timeline**: By when?
6. **Support**: What help will you provide?
7. **Measurement**: How will progress be tracked?

**Development Activities:**
- Training courses
- Mentoring relationships
- Stretch assignments
- Pair work with experts
- Self-study resources
- Conference attendance
- Certification programs
- Job shadowing

### Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Understanding what motivates people helps you support performance:

**Hygiene Factors** (Prevent Dissatisfaction):
- Salary
- Job security
- Working conditions
- Company policies
- Relationships with colleagues

**Motivators** (Create Satisfaction):
- Achievement
- Recognition
- The work itself
- Responsibility
- Advancement
- Growth

**Implication**: Fixing hygiene factors won't motivate‚Äîbut ignoring them will demotivate. True motivation comes from meaningful work, recognition, and growth opportunities.

   Retrospective actions must happen. Schedule follow-up on sprint goal patterns.

4. **Process Change:**
   Implement PR comment SLA or review process.

---

## Summary: Document Detective Skills

When analyzing project artifacts, look for:

| Signal Type | Examples | PM Response |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| Performance decline | Velocity drop, missed commitments | Investigate root cause |
| Collaboration gaps | Delayed help, ignored requests | Address team norms |
| Unheard voices | Repeated concerns, "on record" language | Create psychological safety |
| Hidden contributions | PR comments, behind-scenes work | Recognize invisible work |
| Burnout indicators | Sick time, disengagement, cynicism | Check in on wellness |
| Pattern vs incident | "Again," repeated themes | Address systemically |

# TAB 8: FEEDBACK TYPE MATCHER (Relationship Matcher)

```markdown
‚Üê Back
Support Team Performance
Feedback Type Matcher

Match Situations to Appropriate Feedback Approaches

Welcome to Feedback Type Matcher! In this activity, you'll match workplace situations to the most appropriate feedback type, timing, and delivery method. This builds your ability to select the right approach for performance conversations.

---

## Feedback Types Reference

Before matching, review these feedback categories:

### By Purpose:
| Type | When to Use | Focus |
|------|-------------|-------|
| **Positive/Reinforcing** | Behavior you want repeated | Strengths, successes |
| **Constructive/Developmental** | Behavior that needs to change | Growth, improvement |
| **Coaching** | Building capability | Skills, development |
| **Corrective** | Addressing problems | Issues, consequences |

### By Timing:
| Type | When to Use | Advantage |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Immediate** | Right after event | Highest impact, clear connection |
| **Scheduled** | Regular 1-on-1s | Thoughtful, not reactive |
| **Periodic** | Monthly/quarterly reviews | Patterns, progress |
| **Event-based** | After projects/milestones | Context-specific |

### By Delivery:
| Type | When to Use | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Public** | Positive recognition | Team meeting shout-out |
| **Private** | Constructive feedback | 1-on-1 conversation |
| **Written** | Documentation needed | Email after verbal |
| **Verbal** | Most feedback | Direct conversation |

---

## MATCHING EXERCISE

For each situation, select:
1. Feedback PURPOSE
2. Feedback TIMING
3. Feedback DELIVERY

---

### Situation 1:
**Developer's code caused a production incident. They've already fixed it. This is the first time.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Constructive** (not punitive‚Äîfirst incident)
- Timing: **Same day, after resolution** (timely but not in crisis)
- Delivery: **Private verbal** (then document if needed)

**SBI Example:**
"After the release yesterday, your code change caused a 2-hour outage affecting 500 customers. I know you fixed it quickly. Let's discuss what we can do differently in testing to prevent this."

---

### Situation 2:
**Team member went above and beyond to help a colleague meet a deadline by staying late.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive/Reinforcing**
- Timing: **Immediate** (same or next day)
- Delivery: **Private first, ask about public** (respect preference)

**SBI Example:**
"Yesterday when Jordan was struggling to meet the deadline, you stayed late to help them finish. That collaboration allowed us to deliver on time and showed real team spirit. Would you be comfortable if I shared this in the sprint review?"

---

### Situation 3:
**Team member's work has been declining for 3 weeks. You've had one prior conversation with no improvement.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Corrective** (escalation from constructive)
- Timing: **Scheduled 1-on-1** (serious conversation needs dedicated time)
- Delivery: **Private, then documented**

**SBI Example:**
"Since our conversation three weeks ago, I've noticed the quality issues have continued‚Äîwe've had 5 more bugs reach production. I want to understand what's getting in the way and create a specific improvement plan with clear milestones."

---

### Situation 4:
**Junior developer asks for feedback on their first major feature delivery.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Coaching** (developmental focus)
- Timing: **Scheduled shortly after delivery** (while fresh but not rushed)
- Delivery: **Private verbal** (safe space for growth discussion)

**SBI Example:**
"Your authentication feature shipped on time and passed all tests. That's a strong first major delivery. Let me share what I noticed went well: [specifics]. For growth, here's what to focus on next time: [specifics]."

---

### Situation 5:
**Team member publicly embarrassed a colleague by criticizing their work in a meeting.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Corrective** (behavior issue, not performance)
- Timing: **Same day, after meeting** (can't let it sit)
- Delivery: **Private verbal first**

**SBI Example:**
"In today's planning meeting, when you said 'that approach is completely wrong' in front of the team, Sarah looked visibly embarrassed and shut down for the rest of the meeting. We need to discuss how we give feedback on this team."

---

### Situation 6:
**High performer delivers consistent excellence quarter after quarter. You haven't said much because there are no issues.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive** (proactive recognition)
- Timing: **Now‚Äîdon't wait** (you've been neglecting them)
- Delivery: **Private first, then public**

**SBI Example:**
"I realized I haven't told you this directly: for the past three quarters, you've consistently delivered the highest quality work on the team. Your architecture decisions have saved us weeks of rework. I want you to know I see it and value it."

---

### Situation 7:
**Team member has a skill gap that's slowing their work‚Äînot a motivation issue.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Coaching** (development, not criticism)
- Timing: **Scheduled 1-on-1** (needs thoughtful plan)
- Delivery: **Private verbal**

**SBI Example:**
"I've noticed the Kubernetes deployments take you longer than expected. That's totally normal‚Äîit's new technology for you. Let's create a development plan to build that skill. Would pairing with Maya for the next few deployments help?"

---

### Situation 8:
**Team successfully completed a challenging sprint with high collaboration.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Positive** (team recognition)
- Timing: **End of sprint/immediately** (don't wait)
- Delivery: **Public** (team achievement = team recognition)

**SBI Example:**
"Team, this sprint we faced unexpected scope changes mid-sprint and still delivered the core features. Everyone adjusted, helped each other, and stayed focused. That adaptability is exactly what high-performing teams do."

---

### Situation 9:
**You overheard a team member complaining about you to a colleague.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Curiosity first** (understand before responding)
- Timing: **After cooling off** (don't react emotionally)
- Delivery: **Private verbal**

**SBI Example:**
"I heard something about frustration with how I handled [situation]. I'd like to understand your perspective‚Äîcan you help me understand what would have worked better?"

---

### Situation 10:
**New team member is struggling but hasn't asked for help‚Äîseems embarrassed.**

**Best Match:**
- Purpose: **Supportive/Coaching**
- Timing: **Proactive 1-on-1** (don't wait for them to ask)
- Delivery: **Private, low-pressure**

**SBI Example:**
"I remember when I was new‚Äîit's tough to know when to ask for help. I've noticed the sprint work is taking longer than expected. That's completely normal in a new environment. What would help you ramp up faster?"

---

## DECISION FRAMEWORK

When deciding feedback approach, ask:

```
1. What's the PURPOSE?
   - Reinforce? ‚Üí Positive
   - Develop? ‚Üí Coaching
   - Change behavior? ‚Üí Constructive
   - Address serious issue? ‚Üí Corrective

2. What's the TIMING?
   - How urgent?
   - How emotional?
   - How serious?
   - When is the person receptive?

3. What's the DELIVERY?
   - Positive + Person comfortable public? ‚Üí Public
   - Constructive or corrective? ‚Üí ALWAYS Private
   - Needs documentation? ‚Üí Written follow-up
```

---

## COMMON MISTAKES

| Mistake | Problem | Better Approach |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Public corrective feedback | Humiliates, damages trust | Always private for issues |
| Delayed positive feedback | Loses impact | Recognize within 24 hours |
| Only negative feedback | Demotivates | Balance with recognition |
| Vague feedback | Not actionable | Use SBI for specifics |
| Feedback sandwich | Feels manipulative | Be direct and respectful |
| Assuming one approach fits all | Different people need different styles | Know your team members |
```
